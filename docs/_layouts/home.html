About

arcologies is an interactive environment for designing 2d sound arcologies with monome grid and Ableton Live. It works like this:

  • Arrange cells on a field.
  • Change a cell's structure to define behavior.
  • Open a cell's ports to create leylines.
  • Signals strongerge from hive cells.
  • Signals play a cell's note when the two collide.
  • Finally, depending on how the colliding cell is configured, the signal could be re-routed, split, or destroyed.

It is an M4L midi instrument that pairs especially well with drum racks and zoned instrument racks.

Inspired by tools like meadowphysics, Orca, and Sunvox. But also other curios like Dwarf Fortress, the tower defense genre, and the Eno / Chilvers suite of generative applications.

Also carbon-based life, water, the anthropocene, supply chains, The Mushroom at the End of the World, acrologies, The Dark Mountain Project, Eco Futurism Corp, "seasteading", the gedankenexperiment-prompt: "sound artist's work in a William Gibson novel?", Conway's Game of Life, Garfield Park Conservatory, the Epcot Center, and Buckminster Fuller. And, of course, actual arcologies and metabolism.

Installation

Double click the M4L device to install it.

The demo `.als` was built with Live 10. It will not work with prior versions of Live.

Add the device to a new midi track and add any instrument to the track. Synths are an easy way to always get some sounds going fast. Drum racks require more configuration to line up all the notes. The most advanced usage probably involves zoned intrument racks. See the demo set included in the download for examples.

arcologies was built for grid 128 but is lovingly compatible with 64 and 256. Just select your edition and click reset.

Concepts

Term Definition
Field This is your canvas. Your grid is now a field where cells live, leylines wind, and signals propagate. The field encapsulates everything except the quick menu.
Quick Menu The first column of your grid. For doing menu-y things.
Cell The foundational building block of arcologies. Stationary structures on the grid. Cells have many attributes.
Structure Each cell has one-and-only-one structure. There are three types: hive, gate, shrine. But cells can change structure.
Hive Births signals at the rate of the metabolism.
Gate Stops or allows signals to pass (% = 0) of the metabolism.
Shrine Delay signals for the length of the metabolism.
Metabolism Each cell has a metabolism. The metabolism determines the rate of the cells behavior.
Note Gate and shrines play their note during a signal collision. Hives play theirs on during signal birth.
Signal Travels between cells, typically on leylines. Emit midi when they collide with cells.
Leylines Glowing line segments that connect cells or terminate at the field boundary. Leylines help you visualize your arcology. They have no further function.
Port Each cell has four ports: north, east, south, west. Each ports is either open or closed.

Cells

Cells are the building blocks of arcologies. In addition to their x / y coordinates, cells have four key components:

  1. Ports
  2. Structure
  3. Note
  4. Metabolism

Ports

Ports direct the flow of signals. Each cell has four ports: north, east, south, west. Open ports allow signals in. Closed ports stop signals.

Note you can only view/edit the route of one cell at a time.

Ports & Metabolism

Hives emit signals from all open ports at a rate as defined by their metabolism.

Gates block signals at a rate defined by their metabolism. A gate with a metabolism of "1" would let every signal through. A gate with a metabolism of "2" would let every second signal through, and so on. Blocked signals are destroyed.

Shrines work like gates except that instead of blocking signals, they retain signals. So a shrine with a metabolism of "1" would let every signal through with no delay. But with a metabolism of "2", it would hold onto each incoming signal for two beats.

Ports & Signal Flow

Gates and shrines route incoming signals to every other open port.

Structure

Each active cell has one structure. Structure is configured via in the quick menu. Newly activated cells default to whatever global structure is set. Hive is the default.

Note

Each cell has one midi note. This note will play when a signal collides with the cell, or (in the case of hives) the signals are birthed. Presently, arcologies supports the chromatic scale of C1 - F#5.

Metabolism

Metabolism is the rate at which hives birth signals, gates block signals, and shrines delay signals.

Metabolism is synced with Live's playback.

Consider an arcology in 4/4 time. This table illustrates the rates at which hives birth signals:

Interval Frequency Notation
6 (slowest) Every 6 beats Dotted whole note
5 Every 5 beats Whole note tied with a quarter note
4 Every 4 beats Whole note
3 Every 3 beats Dotted half note
2 Every other beat Half note
1(fastest) One per beat Quarter note

Signals

Signals have a position (x & y) and a direction (n, e, s, or w).

If a signal collides into the side of a cell without an open route, the signal disappears into silence (the poor little signal just smashed into a wall.) The same is true if it wanders off the field. The same is true if they collide with a hive. If a channel disappears from beneath a signal, the signal will continue along. Perhaps you can help it will find its way again?

Signal Collisions

If two (or more) signals land at the same place at the same time -- either in the field or at a cell -- both will be destroyed. However, signals may "pass" through each other so long as they wouldn't land at the same cell on the same beat.

Channels

Channels are defined by cell routes. Signals flow through channels. Channels can cross over each other without consequence.

The Field

At the field's resting state, active cells (hives, ports, nomads) will be displayed with a single, brightly illuminated LED. Signals will be displayed as single, slightly dimmer LEDs, moving about. This is the only state where signal flow can be analyzed.

There are three types of user interaction available on the field:

  1. Single Press
  2. Double Press
  3. Long Press

Note that when a cell is selected, signal flow is hidden.

Single Press

  • Single press an inactive cell to activate it. Newly activated default to hives with all routes open.
  • The cell will become selected and its routes and channels will be displayed.
  • Single press the selected cell again to cycle through routes.
  • With the cell still selected, single press any inactive cell to create a new cell.

Double Press

  • With a cell selected, double press any inactive cell to move it to that cell.

Long Press

  • Long press any active cell to open the menu for that cell.
  • If a cell is selected, a long press on any empty cell will deselect it and display signal flow again.
  • Long pressing any inactive cell is reserved for future functionality.

The Menu

Long press any active cell to open the menu:

Pressing any dimly lit key will close the menu.

Menu Detail

The four cell attributes (route, structure, note, interval) may be configured in the menu.

Cycle through routes as you would on the field. Select one of three structures (hive, port, nomad). Choose notes with the midi palette.

Finally, you may delete cells with the bottom row of the menu. There is no "confirm" action. Fortune favors the brave.

Midi Palette

The midi palette contains the notes C3-B5. Press any key to set the note.

Pressing any dimly lit key will close the midi palette.

Example

Consider this simple arcology of one hive and three ports.

Let us assume the hive is set to C4, with an interval of three (i.e. whole notes.) It has a "North East South" route.

Let us further assume each of the ports are set to F4 with one directional routes pointing back to the hive.

What happens when we engage Live's playback?

Beat 1 (downbeat)

First, the hive will emit three signals: one each to the north, east, and south. The hive will send one midi note (C4) to Live. We'll see the three signals begin traveling each of channels. (Note you cannot view both channels and signal flow at the same time... yet.)

Beat 2

Signals always move at a rate of 1 cell per quarter note, so on beat 2, the northern signal will collide with the port. It will send midi note F4 to Live. Since this is a dead-end port, the signal is destroyed.

Beat 3

The eastern and southern signals will continue down their channels.

Beat 4

The southern signal will collide with the port. It will send midi note F4 to Live. Again, since this is a dead-end port, the signal is destroyed.

Beat 5

The eastern signal will collide with the port, with the same results as the previous two signals... but it is also time for the hive to emit another cluster of signals! The result is three new singals entering the field from the hive, one leaving at the eastern port, and the dyad of C4 (via hive) & F5 (via eastern port) being sent to Live.

Philosophy

arcologies is an instrument for exploration and discovery. At its core it is designed to be a deterministic compositional tool. Mastery of any medium depends on both familiarity and consistent response. This philosophy extends through all the code of arcologies. If a certain combination of events occur, the context that gave rise to their emergence ought to be reproducible with 100% accuracy. With a given configuration of cells, structures, notes, (and eventually nomads), entire songs could emerge and be replayed note-for-note from a relatively simple set of rules.

All the data is stored as JSON and importing/exporting/sharing arcologies is on the roadmap.

The "random" cell route completely undermines this above philophy, but it sure is fun.

Happy hacking.
